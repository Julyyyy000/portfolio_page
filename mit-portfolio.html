Dreamcore

Two-Folio Interactive Pop-Up Installation

November 2025

Medium
Hand-drawn illustration, paper engineering, digital editing, inkpen & acrylic marker craft, Arduino IDE (ESP32), color sensor, rotary input, TFT display; video + still images
Role
Solo-developed — concept, illustration, paper engineering, electronics, programming

Project Overview

Dreamcore explores liminal spaces as psychological safe zones—quiet, transitional environments that feel both familiar and unreal. The project takes the form of a two-folio interactive pop-up installation that blends hand-crafted paper engineering with responsive digital systems.

All environments were hand-drawn, digitally edited, and printed onto 100lb paper to preserve tactile ink texture while accommodating embedded electronics. Rather than presenting narrative through a screen alone, Dreamcore asks whether an analog, handcrafted world—augmented through Arduino-based interaction—can evoke the uncanny softness, nostalgia, and ambiguity associated with dreamcore aesthetics.

The first folio opens to an empty classroom and a lone bicycle selling balloons. A color sensor connected to an ESP32 microcontroller detects the viewer’s interaction: when a balloon object is placed “outside” the window, animated balloons drift into a dreamlike sky displayed on a TFT screen. The interaction is intentionally simple, allowing a single physical gesture to produce a quiet but emotionally resonant response.

Dreamcore — Folio 1 Stills

The second folio reveals fading storefronts and a silent movie theater. Using a rotary input, viewers can tilt the TFT display toward themselves—confronting their own presence within the emptiness—or watch a half-forgotten moment: a kiddie ride outside an abandoned shop slowly rocking on its own. These interactions are designed to feel restrained rather than instructional, encouraging reflection rather than task-based engagement.

Dreamcore — Folio 2 Stills

Dreamcore integrates pop-up paper structures, origami-based mechanisms, hand illustration, and physical computing into a single system. I worked with the Arduino IDE on an ESP32 microcontroller, integrating color sensors, rotary inputs, and TFT displays with Processing-based visuals.

Building the project required repeated cycles of cutting, folding, measuring, coding, redrawing, and rebuilding. Pop-up stability, sensor reliability, wiring layout, and screen placement were tested and revised through multiple iterations. The physical constraints of paper engineering directly informed the electronic system design, requiring careful coordination between structure, circuitry, and code.

Through this process, I developed a workflow that balances precision with openness—designing interactive systems that are structurally sound while remaining emotionally ambiguous. Dreamcore ultimately reinforced my interest in creating hybrid environments where meaning emerges through the tension between analog craft and digital response.

Safe Space

Interactive Projection-Mapped Installation

May 2023

Medium
Projection mapping, clay sculpture, wood construction, digital animation, TouchDesigner, keyboard-based interaction, sound design
Role
Solo-developed — concept, physical fabrication, animation, interaction design, system programming

Project Overview

Safe Space is an interactive projection-mapped installation that explores how anxiety and uncertainty can destabilize one’s sense of reality—even within spaces typically associated with comfort and safety. The work takes place inside a miniature bedroom constructed from wooden boards and clay-made furniture, onto which animated imagery and sound are projected.

The project centers on a character who lives within this room. As viewers interact with different objects, the memories associated with those objects change, and the character responds through shifting facial expressions and breathing patterns. While a bedroom is often understood as a quiet getaway, Safe Space challenges this assumption by revealing how internal doubt and cognitive instability can transform even the most familiar environments into sources of distress.

Rather than telling a fixed narrative, the installation invites viewers to experience a gradual emotional shift—from curiosity to unease—through interaction, sound, and visual disruption.

The physical environment was intentionally handcrafted. Furniture and the character were sculpted from paper clay, creating neutral, unfinished surfaces that function as blank canvases for projected memory. Early prototypes explored 3D-printed forms and Unity-based projection, but these were abandoned in favor of clay and wood, which better supported the project’s intimacy and emotional tone.

Projection mapping required extensive experimentation. I worked with multiple projector configurations—eventually combining a front-facing projector with a top-down projection—to ensure that imagery could inhabit both architectural surfaces and small object details. A physical clay character replaced early animated character concepts to avoid visual distortion during projection.

Safe Space — Fabrication & Mapping Setup

User testing revealed a consistent emotional arc. Participants often described the space as “cute” or calm at first, followed by discomfort, stress, and concern for the character. Feedback confirmed that the gradual loss of control—rather than sudden disruption—was key to the work’s emotional impact. The project also revealed technical limitations, including fragility during transport and sensitivity to prolonged interaction, which informed my understanding of scalability and robustness in interactive installations.

Safe Space represents my interest in creating environments where interaction is not about solving a problem, but about inhabiting an emotional condition—using system behavior, physical materials, and audience input to communicate experiences that are difficult to articulate verbally.

Each piece of furniture in the room functions as an interactive trigger. Viewers use a handheld keyboard to activate different objects, prompting changes in projected imagery and sound. Initially, interactions feel controlled and exploratory: objects shuffle gently, and the character breathes calmly.

However, sustained or repeated input causes the system to destabilize. Projections accelerate, audio glitches intensify, and the character’s breathing becomes rapid and panicked. When multiple objects are activated simultaneously, the room becomes chaotic—mirroring the experience of intrusive thoughts and cognitive overload.

The system was built in TouchDesigner using keyboard input to control video playback, animation states, and sound layers. Rather than rewarding mastery, the interaction is designed to resist control. The more users attempt to “fix” or stabilize the space, the more unstable it becomes—reflecting cycles of doubt and anxiety that escalate through repeated checking and rumination.

Night By Exam

2D Narrative Puzzle Game

November 2024

Medium
Unity 2D game, original pixel art, narrative puzzle design
Role
Solo-developed — concept, programming, art, narrative design, production

Project Overview

Night By Exam is a 2D pixel-art narrative puzzle game that explores exam anxiety, intrusive thoughts, and the emotional pressure surrounding academic performance. Developed for the 2024 Mental Health Champion game jam, the game uses looping environments and narrative-driven puzzles to depict cycles of self-criticism and avoidance.

Players control a protagonist preparing for an exam late at night. Although the exit from the room is physically accessible, the player repeatedly remains inside, navigating familiar objects and fragmented notes. This looping structure reflects the difficulty of confronting fear directly, and the tendency to stay within known routines even when they become distressing.

Rather than dramatizing mental illness, the game presents anxiety as a quiet, internal struggle—expressed through environmental repetition, subtle visual changes, and restrained narrative cues.

Video Demo

The game is built around traditional point-and-click mechanics combined with a looping spatial system. Each loop reveals small environmental changes: notes shift, objects gain new meanings, and dialogue fragments evolve. These changes signal the protagonist’s emotional state rather than advancing a conventional plot.

Core systems were implemented in Unity, including room-reset logic, interaction states, puzzle progression, and narrative triggers. Visual tone and pacing were carefully tuned to maintain a sense of quiet tension without overwhelming the player. Pixel art assets, character animations, and UI elements were all created by hand to maintain stylistic cohesion.

Developing Night By Exam strengthened my understanding of how mechanics, visual language, and narrative structure can work together to communicate psychological states. Working solo required balancing programming, art production, and narrative pacing within a short time frame, reinforcing my ability to prototype, iterate, and polish interactive systems efficiently. The project reflects my ongoing interest in using games as a medium for translating internal emotional experiences into interactive form with nuance and care.

Missing Qin

2D Narrative Puzzle Game

April 2025

Medium
Unity 2D game, original pixel art, narrative puzzle design
Role
Team of 2 — background art, programming, and interaction design

Project Overview

Missing Qin is a 2D pixel-art narrative puzzle game that examines how harassment and social pressure are often normalized or romanticized within school environments. Developed for Ludum Dare 57 by a two-person team, the game follows a protagonist searching for a missing classmate, gradually uncovering how institutional silence and peer dynamics contribute to harm.

Rather than framing the mystery around a single event, the game reveals patterns of behavior through environmental storytelling. As players explore familiar school spaces, they encounter fragmented dialogue that builds up to a whole story. The narrative intentionally avoids clear villains, focusing instead on the collective systems that allow harm to persist.

The project explores how memory, rumor, and social expectation shape what is considered “normal,” along with topics of how friendship persists under high pressure.
